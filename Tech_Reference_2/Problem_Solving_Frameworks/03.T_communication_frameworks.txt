##list commonly used communication frameworks with a short summary

Here are the most popular communication frameworks used globally to structure feedback, deliver presentations, resolve conflicts, and pitch ideas clearly:

1. SCQA (Situation-Complication-Question-Answer) 
A storytelling and business communication framework developed by McKinsey & Company to structure complex information into a compelling narrative. 
    • Situation: State the current, undisputed facts or context.
    • Complication: Introduce the trigger, bottleneck, or problem that disrupted the situation.
    • Question: Formulate the core question or challenge that arises from the complication.
    • Answer: Deliver your proposed solution or recommendation directly.

2. Radical Candor
A feedback framework developed by Kim Scott that builds high-performing teams by balancing professional challenge with personal care. 
    • Care Personally: Show genuine empathy, investment, and human connection with the individual.
    • Challenge Directly: Provide clear, upfront, and unvarnished truth about their performance gaps.
    • The Goal: Avoid ruinous empathy (caring without challenging) and obnoxious aggression (challenging without caring). 

3. STAR Method (Situation-Task-Action-Result)
A universally adopted framework for behavioral interviews, status reports, and structured storytelling.
    • Situation: Briefly describe the specific context, challenge, or background.
    • Task: Outline the objective, responsibility, or target you needed to achieve.
    • Action: Explain exactly what you did, how you did it, and the tools you used.
    • Result: Detail the outcome, quantified metrics, and lessons learned from the action. 

4. Nonviolent Communication (NVC)
Developed by Marshall Rosenberg, this framework resolves personal or professional conflicts by focusing on objective observations and shared needs. [1, 2]
    • Observations: State the objective, concrete facts of what happened without judgement.
    • Feelings: Express how you feel in relation to these facts.
    • Needs: Identify the core human needs or values creating those feelings.
    • Requests: Make a specific, actionable, and concrete request to move forward. 

5. PREP Technique 
A punchy framework designed for impromptu speaking, answering executive questions, or making a quick point in a meeting.
    • Point: State your main message or conclusion in the very first sentence.
    • Reason: Give the underlying data, logic, or justification for your point.
    • Explanation: Provide a concrete example, story, or statistic to illustrate it.
    • Point: Restate your original point to close the loop and solidify the message.
