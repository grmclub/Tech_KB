In this video, Mike Gibbs outlines the seven critical skills required to succeed as an Enterprise Architect in 2026. He emphasizes that the role is less about technical frameworks and more about making strategic decisions that align technology with business goals to drive value.

The Seven Key Skills for Enterprise Architects:

    Technical Knowledge (0:30-3:53): Serving as the foundation, this involves understanding how various technology domains (cloud, security, AI, etc.) fit together to solve business problems, rather than just deep-diving into engineering tasks.
    Business Acumen (3:54-6:44): Architects must understand how an organization makes money, its operating models, and how to frame technical needs as business solutions to create competitive advantage.
    Executive Presence and Relevancy (6:45-9:33): Developing the ability to communicate with senior leadership in a way that is clear, calm, and focused on outcomes, building the necessary trust for strategic influence.
    Sales Skills (9:34-12:24): The capacity to persuade and influence stakeholders to secure funding and sponsorship for architecture initiatives, effectively "selling" the business value of a vision.
    Leadership Skills (12:25-14:55): Leading cross-functional teams and managing transformation initiatives through influence and guidance, often without having formal authority over all involved parties.
    Communication Skills (14:56-17:27): Translating complex technical details into meaningful business language to ensure alignment across different departments and clear decision-making.
    Stakeholder Management (17:28-19:41): Identifying and balancing the needs, fears, and constraints of various stakeholders to ensure the architecture is actually adopted and not blocked by organizational resistance.

Key Takeaway:
Enterprise Architects are paid for their decisions, not their deployments. The core of the profession is the discipline of aligning business strategy, technology, people, and governance to help organizations grow and innovate.

According to the speaker, stakeholders may not always explicitly state their true motivations or the underlying nature of a problem during meetings. Instead, they often present a symptom of a larger issue—for instance, requesting a new system when the real problem is actually a poor process, fragmented data ownership, or unclear decision rights (18:40 - 19:04). Additionally, the video notes that stakeholders have varying needs, expectations, fears, and constraints that they may not fully disclose, which can lead to them quietly resisting, delaying funding for, or bypassing architectural standards if they feel their specific concerns have not been addressed (17:51 - 17:56, 19:23 - 19:33).

This video, presented by Go Cloud Architects, defines the true archetype of an Enterprise Architect (EA), emphasizing that the role is primarily an executive-level position rather than just a technical one. The core message is that successful EAs act as strategy enablers, business translators, and change leaders rather than just focusing on diagrams or system implementation.
Key Skills for an Enterprise Architect:

    Strategy (1:02): EAs focus on "strategy in motion," translating business visions into execution realities and focusing on driving business outcomes.
    Business Acumen (2:00): A non-negotiable skill. EAs must understand revenue, risk, cost structures, and ROI to ensure technology provides a competitive advantage.
    Communication (2:59): The ability to distill complexity and adapt messages to different audiences—from technical teams to the C-suite—is crucial for project alignment.
    Sales and Influence (4:48): EAs must sell decisions to executives by presenting options, consequences, and clear business value.
    CXO Relevance (6:20): An EA must be able to speak the language of various executives (e.g., CFOs care about ROI, CISOs care about risk/resilience).

    Consulting Mindset (11:17): Effective EAs frame problems, perform root cause analysis, and manage ambiguity to guide leadership.
    Technical Fundamentals (12:31): While not a hands-on engineering role, EAs need broad technical literacy (networking, cloud, security, data) to maintain credibility and make informed, risk-based architectural judgments.

Why EA is a Top Career:

Enterprise architecture remains highly stable and AI-resilient because it sits at the intersection of business strategy, technology enablement, and organizational decision-making (14:50). Unlike engineering, which focuses on how to build, architecture focuses on what should be built and why, based on long-term business goals and judgment.

This video, presented by enterprise architect Mike Gibbs, explains the concept of an Enterprise Architecture Operating Model—a blueprint that aligns an organization's business strategy with its technology, data, and processes to effectively deliver value (0:57 - 2:56).
Key Components of an Operating Model

An effective operating model bridges the gap between high-level business strategy and technical execution (3:00 - 4:13). It encompasses:

    Organizational Structure: Roles, teams, and hierarchies.
    Business Processes: Standardized workflows for operations and sales.
    Data & Application Architecture: How information is stored, accessed, and used across the organization.
    Technology Architecture: Infrastructure (servers, cloud, network).

The Four Types of Operating Models

Mike details four primary models based on the need for process standardization and system integration (4:25 - 9:20):

    Diversification (4:26): For conglomerates or holding companies where business units function independently with different processes and systems (e.g., General Electric).
    Replication (5:35): For franchises or chains that require standardized business processes but do not necessarily need integrated IT systems (e.g., McDonald's).
    Coordination (6:44): For organizations that need to share data and technology across units but allow local business units to perform their own unique processes (e.g., Financial Services).
    Unification (8:02): For businesses requiring global standardization of both processes and systems (e.g., Airlines).

Choosing the Right Model

To determine the best fit for your enterprise, consider these guidelines (9:21 - 10:44):

    Same customers, shared systems? Choose Unification.
    Shared systems, different processes? Choose Coordination.
    Same business model, independent operations? Choose Replication.
    Different business models and customers? Choose Diversification.

The video concludes by emphasizing that these models are essential for strategic clarity and ensuring that IT investments directly support business goals rather than just adopting 'trendy' technology.

##Enterprise Architect vs Platform Architect vs Solutions Architect (Explained Clearly)

This video, presented by Mike Gibbs from Go Cloud Architects, clarifies the distinct responsibilities, focus areas, and executive-level success metrics for three major architecture roles to help professionals avoid career-stalling mistakes.
1. Enterprise Architect (EA)

    Focus: The entire business, including strategy, operating models, capabilities, and governance (2:11-2:36).
    Goal: Aligning technology with business outcomes and long-term (3-5 year) goals rather than specific cloud services (2:36-2:55).
    Success Metrics: Reduced technology sprawl, decreased risk/attack footprint, and clear executive roadmaps (3:26-3:57).

2. Platform Architect

    Focus: The foundations the business runs on (e.g., cloud, security, or networking infrastructure) (1:20-1:39).
    Goal: Designing for scale, consistency, and reuse to avoid chaos and configuration drift (5:30-6:07).
    Success Metrics: Faster team onboarding, fewer security exceptions, and lower operational friction (6:13-6:37).

3. Solutions Architect (SA)

    Focus: Delivery for a single workload, specific application, or architecture pattern (1:44-1:54, 6:52-6:56).
    Goal: Turning the direction set by EA and platform architects into real, technical implementations (7:44-8:02).
    Success Metrics: On-time delivery, meeting functional/non-functional requirements, and avoiding long-term technical debt (8:32-8:55).

Key Takeaways for Career Advancement

    The Critical Mistake: Transitioning to a senior architecture role but continuing to do lower-level tasks (e.g., an Enterprise Architect doing Solutions Architect work) (9:00-9:13).
    Executive Mindset: Every architecture role is a form of business leadership; success depends on focusing on the specific scope of your domain rather than getting trapped in tactical execution outside of your responsibility (9:24-9:57).

#How to Think Like a Cloud Architect, Not a Cloud Engineer

This video, presented by Mike Gibbs of Go Cloud Careers, explains the essential mindset shift required for professionals transitioning from Cloud Engineer to Cloud Architect roles.
Core Philosophy: Business vs. Technical Focus

    Cloud Engineers are primarily tasked with execution: building, configuring, and operating technical systems (0:25, 1:33).
    Cloud Architects are tasked with strategy: solving business problems and creating tangible business value (0:30, 1:41).

The 3 Key Questions for Architects (0:56)

Architects are hired to answer these fundamental questions rather than just selecting tools:

    What should the business build?
    Why should the business build it?
    How will the design create actual business value (e.g., increased revenue, cost reduction, or risk mitigation)?

The Cloud Architect Thinking Framework (4:05)

To succeed in interviews and professional roles, architects should approach problems in this specific order:

    Business Goals: What is the company trying to achieve?
    Constraints: Budget, timeline, compliance, and talent availability.
    Risks: What could fail, damage reputation, or cause loss?
    Capabilities: What must the business be able to do post-architecture?
    Trade-offs: What are you optimizing for (e.g., speed vs. stability vs. cost)?
    Architecture Strategy: Defining target states and guardrails.
    Technology Choices: The final step is selecting the tools (AWS, Azure, Kubernetes, etc.) to fit the strategy.

Practical Tips for Career Growth

    Interview Mindset: Avoid talking exclusively about tools or services. Instead, discuss how you align technology with business outcomes (8:40).
    Communication: Shift your language from "I know how to build X" to "I know how to design cloud strategies that improve speed, reduce risk, and support growth" (8:43-9:10).

##Enterprise Architecture Fundamentals Bootcamp: What Enterprise Architects Actually Do Day 2

In this Enterprise Architecture Fundamentals Bootcamp (Day 2), instructor Mike Gibbs provides a practical deep dive into what enterprise architects actually do, focusing on moving from theory to tangible business value. The session demystifies key architecture concepts, emphasizes avoiding "framework theater," and demonstrates how to apply TOGAF principles in a real-world case study.
Key Topics Covered:

    Reference vs. Solutions Architecture (0:05:49 - 0:26:13): Mike explains that a reference architecture acts as a standardized "recipe pattern" for the enterprise, while a solutions architecture is the specific implementation ("the meal") tailored to a project with exact vendor components and sizing.
    Architecture Frameworks (0:46:07 - 1:05:21): The discussion highlights that frameworks like TOGAF are tools, not rulebooks. The goal is to avoid "framework theater"—where teams generate excessive documentation without outcomes—by focusing on lightweight, consumable artifacts that support business goals.
    The TOGAF ADM in Practice (1:15:13 - 1:44:15): The session provides a practical walkthrough of the TOGAF Architecture Development Method (ADM), breaking down phases from Preliminary and Architecture Vision to Business, Data, Application, and Technology Architectures.
    Lab Case Study (1:58:14 - 2:40:37): A live lab activity demonstrates how to apply these concepts by designing a 30-bed freestanding urgent care center. Mike shows the step-by-step process of mapping business capabilities, organizational structure, application/data flows, and finally, the technology and migration roadmap.

Key Takeaways for Aspiring Architects:

    Focus on Business Value: Enterprise architecture is a strategic advisory role. You are there to help the business solve problems and achieve competitive advantage.
    Standardize Thoughtfully: Don't over-standardize. Create reference architectures to prevent reinventing the wheel, but leave room for project-specific solutions.
    Communication is Critical: Architects must act as translators between the C-suite and engineering teams, using plain language and clear, purpose-driven diagrams.

This video, presented by Michael Gibbs of Go Cloud Architects, clarifies the distinct roles within cloud computing to help professionals choose the right career path. The video highlights how the roles differ in their focus on technical depth, business strategy, and system design.
Key Roles Defined:

    Cloud Engineer (1:17 - 1:50): The most deeply technical role, focused on the specific configuration and functionality of individual services within a cloud provider (e.g., AWS S3).
    Solutions Architect (2:23 - 4:06): A systems designer who typically works with a single cloud vendor to build end-to-end solutions that optimize business performance. They focus deeply on that specific vendor's ecosystem.
    Cloud Architect (4:28 - 6:14): A broader strategic design role. They act as translators between business executives and engineers, designing global, often multi-cloud or hybrid-cloud solutions to solve complex business challenges.
    Enterprise Architect (6:15 - 7:01): Focuses on the entire enterprise landscape, including applications, security, and infrastructure, with a heavy emphasis on aligning technology with overall business strategy.

Career Guidance:

    Skills Progression: As one moves from a Cloud Engineer to an Enterprise Architect, the need for technical depth in specific services shifts toward a greater emphasis on soft skills, emotional intelligence, executive presence, and business acumen (1:51 - 2:10).
    Educational Resources: Michael Gibbs outlines several free career resources, including webinars for resume building, job interview preparation, and technical deep dives into cloud architecture and networking (7:50 - 9:45).
